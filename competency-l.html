Competency L

Demonstrate understanding of quantitative and qualitative research methods, the ability to design a research project, and the ability to evaluate and synthesize research literature.

Introduction

I’ve always liked figuring things out. Back in my undergradate years, I got comfortable doing research—writing papers, digging through sources, and organizing data to answer specific questions. It felt like a natural process: notice a problem, look into it, and try to make sense of what’s going on. That same curiosity has carried into my work at a public library, and even more so during my time as an MLIS student. I find myself constantly stepping into that research mindset to see how to better serve the community. Why isn’t a program reaching the right audience? What kinds of services are people actually using? Research methods help me approach these everyday questions in a more structured, informed way—whether I’m reviewing existing literature or collecting new data to better understand a situation.

In public libraries, we’re always adjusting services based on community needs. Being able to design small research projects or evaluate existing studies makes those adjustments more thoughtful. This competency is about applying research—not just understanding methods in theory, but using them to improve the way we serve people.

Quantitative and Qualitative Research Methods

Quantitative and qualitative research each bring something different to the table, and both are essential tools for library and information science professionals. Quantitative methods allow us to look at broad patterns—such as program attendance, circulation stats, user demographics, or survey data—and turn those into measurable insights that can inform decisions about how services are delivered (Connaway, Radford, & Powell, 2016). In public libraries, this kind of data is especially helpful when reporting outcomes to funders or city agencies, identifying usage trends, or comparing services across branches. Because quantitative methods rely on numerical data, they also allow for statistical analysis that can highlight correlations, gaps, and patterns that might not be immediately visible (Connaway, Radford, & Powell, 2016).

Qualitative research, meanwhile, helps dig into the “why.” While numbers can tell us what is happening, qualitative data can provide a deeper understanding of user behaviors, motivations, and experiences. Through methods such as interviews, direct observation, focus groups, and open-ended surveys, we gain insight into how people interact with library spaces, programs, and resources (Lincoln, 2002). This kind of research is particularly valuable where services are often shaped by community needs, and success is just as much about relationships and relevance as it is about numbers. Pickard (2013) emphasizes that qualitative methods are especially effective in exploring the social and cultural context of information use, which aligns closely with the mission of community-focused libraries.

The reason why we use both approaches is that quantitative and qualitative gives us a more complete picture together. While quantitative data might show a drop in program attendance, qualitative feedback might reveal that the timing no longer works for families, or that a new instructor isn't connecting with the group. A mixed-methods approach, as Creswell (2009) describes, helps balance the objectivity of numerical data with the insight of lived experiences. In practice, this means that library professionals can make more informed decisions, grounded in both hard evidence and human understanding. For me, learning how to use both approaches has been essential not only in coursework but also in thinking critically about how to improve services in a real-world setting.

Designing a Research Project

Following the public library setting, research questions can often emerge from practice—whether through staff observations, community feedback, or service patterns. Designing a research project begins by clearly articulating the problem or question, followed by a thorough review of the existing literature to understand what has already been explored. Luo, Brancolini, and Kennedy (2017) emphasize the value of connecting research to professional practice and identifying areas where original investigation is necessary.

According to Creswell (2009), strong research design includes the development of a focused research question, a rationale for the selected methodology, and a clear plan for data collection and analysis. The research question acts as a guidepost, informing methodological choices and narrowing the scope of the study. Whether the approach is quantitative, qualitative, or mixed, methods should include both the nature of the question and the library context in which the research is being conducted (Connaway, Radford, & Powell, 2016). This practical alignment makes it so that the project remains feasible and that the findings are applicable and actionable.

Additionally, engaging with the literature at the design stage can uncover methodological frameworks and identify any theoretical gaps that may occur. Luo et al. (2017) note that using existing studies as a foundation helps avoid duplication while revealing opportunities for contribution. By combining professional observations with a literature review, LIS researchers can construct well-informed, manageable studies that are rooted in both evidence and real-world relevance.

Evaluation and Synthesis of Research literature

Engaging with scholarly literature is a core part of the research process, especially when framing a new study or exploring an unfamiliar topic. Evaluation involves more than summarizing—it requires a critical lens focused on methodology, credibility, and relevance. Researchers must consider how a study was conducted, the population involved, and whether its findings are transferable to other contexts (Pickard, 2013). This step is essential in determining the strength and reliability of existing evidence and identifying what gaps remain.

Synthesis builds on that foundation by connecting insights across multiple studies. Rather than viewing sources in isolation, synthesis involves identifying common themes, methodological patterns, and contradictions. This process helps researchers recognize both well-established knowledge and areas that remain underexplored (Welch, Lahr, & Webb, 2021). Using structured organizational strategies—such as thematic mapping or crosswalks—can help manage and interpret large volumes of literature (Matteson & Warren, 2020). These techniques make it easier to detect trends, classify research by setting or audience, and develop a clearer picture of the field’s current state.

Critically evaluating and synthesizing literature not only improves understanding of a topic but also contributes to more thoughtful study design. Recognizing previous research limitations helps avoid repeating mistakes and supports the development of sharper research questions (Connaway, Radford, & Powell, 2016). In the public library context, this process is especially important, as it makes sure that research remains grounded in real-world needs while contributing meaningfully to professional knowledge and practice.

Evidence

COVID-19 Data Visualization (INFO 246)

In my Information Visualization (INFO 246) course, I applied both quantitative and qualitative research methods to analyze COVID-19 data by race and ethnicity in California, using public data from the California Department of Public Health. My role involved creating visualizations with Tableau, such as bar charts and pie graphs, to highlight disparities in infection and mortality rates. Before developing these visualizations, I conducted a literature review to understand how similar public health data had been analyzed, which informed my approach to interpreting the data and framing it within broader health equity discussions. This combination of quantitative data analysis and qualitative literature synthesis deepened my understanding of how to present research findings clearly and meaningfully. The project not only sharpened my skills in data visualization and analysis but also demonstrated the importance of integrating existing research to contextualize and inform new research, an essential competency for designing data-driven library services and programs.


Literature Review Matrix (INFO 200)

My second piece of evidence is a literature review matrix I created for the course Information Communities (INFO 200), which supported my research on the manga reader community. The matrix was designed to help organize scholarly sources and develop a deeper understanding of how information access is shaped by library systems and cataloging structures. Using the template provided in class, I tracked key takeaways from each article, reflected on their relevance to my research, and identified the broader significance of my topic.

This assignment was my first structured experience with evaluating LIS-specific research in an organized, comparative format. The matrix helped me move beyond simply reading articles—it guided me toward identifying trends in metadata practices, surfacing limitations in traditional classification systems like MARC and LCSH, and recognizing emerging models that center user perspectives. As I synthesized this research, I began to formulate a clearer research focus and saw how gaps in the literature could inform practical improvements in how materials like manga are cataloged and discovered. This process gave me hands-on experience in applying evaluative thinking to academic literature, preparing me to design research with a solid grounding in existing work.

Assignment #1: Variables, Measures & Sampling (INFO 285)

My third piece of evidence comes from an assignment in Applied Research Methods (INFO 285). In this project, we evaluated key variables related to library usage, focusing on how spaces and technologies were being utilized at a fictional university library. I created a table outlining four primary variables—library space utilization, visit frequency, technology use, and study room reservations—detailing how each was measured. For example, space utilization was assessed via a checklist survey, while visit frequency was categorized by scale, and technology use through predefined options. The assignment also required me to identify the appropriate level of measurement for each variable, from nominal to ratio, ensuring proper alignment with the research methods.

Additionally, I developed a sampling strategy based on convenience sampling, targeting users who visited the library at specific times and were easily accessible for survey participation. I explained potential biases in this method while aiming for broad representation. This task helped me refine my understanding of research methodologies and the importance of defining variables, selecting measurement techniques, and considering sampling limitations. Completing this assignment strengthened my ability to structure a research project and apply research principles in a real-world context, equipping me with the skills necessary for designing and conducting research that can inform library practices and decision-making.

Assignment #5: Twelve-Month Programming Plan for Maplewood Branch Library (INFO 260A)

For my assignment in Programming and Services for Children (INFO 260A), I developed a Twelve-Month Programming Plan for the fictional Maplewood Branch Library. The project required me to use both quantitative and qualitative research methods to design a set of programs that would meet the community's needs. In the plan, I reviewed demographic data and existing library usage statistics (quantitative) and will conduct interviews with a sample of parents and educators (qualitative). This dual approach intends to allow me to understand the needs of the community from both a statistical and a personal perspective.

In the planning process, I also evaluated relevant research on effective library programming. I synthesized literature on best practices, including community engagement and partnerships, and applied this knowledge to shape the programming plan. This research informed my decisions on program content, scheduling, and outreach strategies, ensuring the plan was both evidence-based and tailored to the community’s needs. Although the Twelve-Month Programming Plan was a theoretical exercise, the assignment helped me demonstrate my ability to integrate quantitative and qualitative research methods into program design. It also showcased my skills in evaluating and synthesizing existing research literature to inform decision-making in a library setting.

Conclusion

Research in public librarianship often starts with a simple question: “What’s going on here?” I would like to think that my background in undergraduate research gave me the tools to approach those questions systematically, and my work in libraries gave me the space to apply them in meaningful ways. Whether I’m reviewing literature to write up a research project or collecting data to redesign a program, I’m always drawing on what I’ve learned about asking clear questions, choosing the right methods, and turning data into insight.

I don’t think that research has to be overwhelming or academic in tone—it can be small-scale, grounded in daily work, and focused on real people. For me, that’s where its value lies. It helps me be more thoughtful and intentional in my role as a public library worker and gives me the confidence to back up decisions with more than just instinct. I believe that it can be one of the most practical tools I will have in public librarianship—and one I plan to keep using, no matter where I work.
