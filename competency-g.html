Competency G

Demonstrate understanding of basic principles and standards involved in organizing information such as classification and controlled vocabulary systems, cataloging systems, metadata schemas or other systems for making information accessible to a particular clientele.

Introduction

Organizing information is essential in library and information science as it allows users to locate and retrieve resources effectively. Standardized systems, such as classification schemes and metadata schemas, provide structured frameworks that enhance accessibility and support global collaboration. These systems promote interoperability across libraries, archives, and other institutions, facilitating seamless discovery and resource sharing (Bolin, 2022). Building on Competency F, which addresses information retrieval, Competency G examines the principles and standards that structure classification systems, controlled vocabularies, cataloging, and metadata.

Classification and Controlled Vocabulary Systems

Classification systems play a crucial role in maintaining consistency in information organization, enabling users to browse and retrieve materials efficiently. The Dewey Decimal Classification (DDC) and Library of Congress Classification (LCC) systems exemplify structured classification approaches used widely in libraries. These systems categorize knowledge into hierarchical structures by facilitating systematic arrangement and retrieval (Lazarinis, 2015). In contrast, controlled vocabularies, such as the Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH) and Medical Subject Headings (MeSH), provide standardized terminologies to maintain uniformity in subject indexing (Lazarinis, 2015). Controlled vocabularies improves precision in search queries by reducing ambiguity and synonym issues; however, they can also pose challenges, such as requiring continuous updates and limiting user-generated terms that may reflect evolving language trends (Weedman, 2023). Comparatively, natural language tagging allows for more flexible and user-friendly search but may result in inconsistencies due to synonym variations and lack of standardization.

Cataloging Systems

Cataloging has evolved significantly to accommodate the growing body of literature and diverse media formats in libraries. The transition from earlier cataloging codes, such as the Anglo-American Cataloging Rules (AACR2), to the Resource Description and Access (RDA) framework demonstrates the field's shift toward greater flexibility and adaptability to digital resources (Lazarinis, 2015). Descriptive cataloging focuses on representing bibliographic details, including titles, authors, and publication information, whereas subject cataloging assigns subject terms that help users find materials on specific topics. Authority control is fundamental in cataloging as well, as it standardizes names and subject headings across records, maintaining consistency in bibliographic databases. Access points, such as author names and subject terms, improve discoverability by linking related records within library catalogs, supporting user search strategies, and improving retrieval accuracy (Bolin, 2022; Wahid, Warraich, & Tahira, 2018).

Metadata Schemas

Metadata, defined as structured information describing resources, is important when we consider its role in both digital and physical collections. The Machine-Readable Cataloging (MARC) format has long been a standard for encoding bibliographic data which allows libraries to share cataloging information efficiently (Lazarinis, 2015). MARC has facilitated interoperability among library systems, allowing for uniform record exchange and integration. However, as digital collections expand, alternative metadata schemas have emerged to address the even more diverse resource types. Dublin Core, for example, provides a simplified metadata standard commonly used for digital libraries and institutional repositories (Oliver & Harvey, 2016). The adoption of multiple metadata schemas reflects the need for specialized approaches in organizing different types of information resources (The Open Archival Information System [OAIS], 2014).

Other Systems

In recent years, library cataloging is shifting toward BIBFRAME, a linked data model designed to replace MARC. By connecting bibliographic entities, BIBFRAME intends to improve resource discovery and enhance interoperability with external data sources (Library of Congress, n.d.). Linked data expands public library catalogs which allows patrons to access broader information networks. However, like any new technology, transitioning to BIBFRAME requires updating tools, training staff, and addressing financial constraints (Wahid, Warraich, & Tahira, 2018). Despite these challenges, metadata-driven systems continue to evolve, striving to help libraries manage their digital collections and community archives effectively.

Evidence

Group Project: Controlled Vocabulary for Target User Group (INFO 202)

In Information Retrieval System Design (INFO 202), I worked on a group project to develop a controlled vocabulary aimed at improving information retrieval for a specific user group. Each team member was responsible for analyzing a portion of sample records, identifying key concepts, and organizing them into term groups. My task involved selecting representative terms while following vocabulary design principles, such as using only nouns, applying correct pluralization, and maintaining consistency in capitalization.

After completing our individual sections, we regrouped to combine our work into a cohesive vocabulary. I contributed by reviewing the collected terms, identifying redundancies, and suggesting adjustments to improve clarity and usability. We engaged in discussions to refine our choices, balancing specificity with accessibility while making necessary revisions to accommodate different perspectives.

This experience highlighted the complexities of controlled vocabulary development and the importance of collaboration in refining terms for practical application. It also deepened my understanding of how widely used vocabularies, like Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH), continuously evolve to incorporate emerging topics and address biases. Through this project, I gained valuable skills in structured information organization and the iterative process of refining controlled vocabularies to support more effective retrieval.

Literature Review Matrix (INFO 200)

My coursework in Information Communities (INFO 200) provided a strong foundation in understanding how different communities interact with information and how library systems organize resources to meet user needs. The research I was doing was trying to understand the information-seeking habits of the manga readers community, and by doing so, I learned how metadata, cataloging standards, and classification systems can possibly impact accessibility.

My literature review matrix serves as a tool for analyzing how metadata schemas, cataloging systems, and classification structures impact the organization and accessibility of manga in libraries. Since manga readers come from diverse backgrounds—ranging from casual fans to scholars—I wanted to explore how they seek, interpret, and categorize information and how library systems can better meet their needs.

One key study, a part of the Literature Review Matrix, Kirayakos and Sugimoto (2019), explores the limitations of traditional bibliographic models like MARC and LCSH in accurately representing manga and other pop culture resources. These systems struggle with serialized storytelling, format variations, and the interconnected nature of manga series. They propose using the Object Reuse and Exchange-Open Archives Initiative (OAI-ORE) model, integrating fan-created metadata for a more flexible bibliographic hierarchy that improves discoverability. Incorporating this study into my literature review matrix helped me critically evaluate alternative metadata structures and understand how classification influences accessibility, reinforcing the need to adapt traditional systems for niche user communities.

Digital Archival Project: Freshwater Aquascaping Styles (INFO 284)

For Digital Curation (INFO 284), I developed a digital archival project using Preservica Starter to organize and preserve a collection on freshwater aquascaping styles. This project emphasized the importance of cataloging and metadata in structuring digital collections for long-term accessibility and user engagement. I applied metadata schemas, such as the Dublin Core Metadata Element Set, to describe and categorize collection items, promoting consistency, discoverability, and interoperability with digital repositories. Each aquascaping style—Nature Aquarium, Iwagumi, Dutch, Biotope, and Forest layouts—was cataloged with standardized metadata elements, facilitating efficient retrieval. I also employed authority control practices to maintain consistency in names and subject terms, particularly when documenting influential aquascapers like Takashi Amano and George Farmer.

In addition to metadata, I focused on classification and controlled vocabulary to improve search precision. By assigning subject headings and keywords, I enhanced the collection's navigability. This experience reinforced my ability to implement cataloging best practices in a digital environment, demonstrating the role of structured information in resource discovery and management. Overall, the project showcased my competency in organizing digital collections, promoting accessibility, and supporting sustainable digital curation practices, emphasizing the evolving role of metadata in information organization.


Conclusion

The organization of information plays a key role in how users access and navigate library resources. The organization of information is central to how users access and navigate library resources, and it's something I’ve come to appreciate more deeply as I explore the digital transformation in libraries. As libraries adapt to the digital age, I see how classification systems, controlled vocabularies, cataloging standards, and metadata schemas must evolve to meet new needs. While traditional models like MARC, DDC, and LCSH have provided a strong foundation, emerging systems such as BIBFRAME and linked data frameworks open up new opportunities to improve discoverability and accessibility. As digital collections expand and user expectations shift, libraries face the challenge of balancing standardization with flexibility to keep their systems functional and inclusive. In this sense, ongoing innovation and research in metadata structures and cataloging practices are essential to advancing information retrieval and supporting diverse communities in today’s digital landscape.
