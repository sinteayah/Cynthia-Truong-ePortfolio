Competency K

Design collaborative or individual learning experiences based on learning principles and theories.

Introduction

As someone working at a public library and aspiring to become a librarian, I’ve come to realize how important it is to understand learning theories and principles when designing effective programs. The goal is not just to provide information, but to create meaningful learning experiences that resonate with patrons. Through my work at the library, I've observed firsthand how different learning styles and needs can influence the way people engage with information. Understanding concepts like constructivism, cognitive load theory, and collaborative learning has helped me think more critically about how library programs can be designed to enhance learning, whether it’s through individual exploration or group-based activities.

By incorporating these theories into instructional design, I believe we can create programs that are accessible, engaging, and responsive to the diverse needs of library users. Whether I’m leading a workshop on using library resources or assisting a patron with a research project, these learning theories will guide me in providing effective, learner-centered experiences that cater to different learning preferences.

Constructivism

Constructivism, as described by Piaget (1970) and Vygotsky (1978), emphasizes that learning is an active process where learners build upon prior knowledge through experience and social interaction. Piaget (1952) suggests that individuals construct their understanding by interacting with the environment, while Vygotsky (1978) focuses on the social aspect of learning, highlighting the importance of the "More Knowledgeable Other" (MKO) in supporting the learner’s development. These ideas stress the importance of active engagement and contextual learning in educational settings, making them particularly relevant in l ibrary instruction.

In practice, constructivism shows up in library instruction through activities that promote problem-solving and real-world application of skills. For example, in an information literacy program, learners might use library resources to solve a specific research problem. This approach encourages learners to actively engage with content, helping them apply new knowledge in a practical context. By emphasizing hands-on activities and real-world challenges, libraries create opportunities for deeper understanding and better retention of skills.

Cognitive Load Theory

Cognitive Load Theory, proposed by Sweller (1988), suggests that instructional design should align with the learner’s cognitive capacity to maximize learning. Sweller (1988) argues that the human brain has limited capacity for processing information, so instructional materials should avoid overwhelming learners with too much content at once. This theory emphasizes the need for well-structured learning experiences that help learners focus on key concepts while minimizing unnecessary cognitive load (Sweller, 1988).

In a library context, cognitive load theory can be applied by designing instruction that breaks down complex information into manageable segments. For example, when teaching citation styles, a library instructor might divide the content into smaller parts, use visual aids like infographics, and include interactive elements such as quizzes. These strategies reduce extraneous cognitive load, allowing learners to concentrate on the essential concepts. Additionally, scaffolding techniques can gradually increase the complexity of the material as learners gain proficiency, helping them avoid feeling overwhelmed by the content.

Collaborative Learning

Collaborative learning, as described by Johnson and Johnson (1999), emphasizes positive interdependence, individual accountability, and promotive interaction—key elements for effective group learning. Their research shows that when learners work together toward shared goals, they develop deeper understanding, stronger critical thinking skills, and greater motivation (Johnson & Johnson, 2005).

In a public library setting, collaborative learning might take place during a digital literacy workshop where participants work in small groups to evaluate websites. Instead of receiving information passively, learners apply criteria, compare findings, and contribute to a shared set of best practices. This structure reflects the principles of cooperation and shared responsibility outlined by Johnson, Johnson, and Holubec (2008).

Digital tools like Google Docs or Padlet can enhance this process by allowing real-time collaboration and knowledge-sharing. By incorporating collaborative learning into library programs, libraries create active learning environments that reflect both the social and educational roles of public spaces.

Evidence

Mad Science Mondays- STEM Workshop Series (San José Public Library)

Open in a new tab

The Mad Science Mondays STEM workshop series is a hands-on program I created, led, and facilitated as a library worker at the San José Public Library. Designed for children ages 6–11, the series introduced scientific concepts like magnetism, density, and chemistry through interactive experiments such as making lava lamps and magnetic slime. I developed this program originally as part of an instructional design plan for INFO 250, and then brought it to life in a real-world public library setting. I was responsible for designing each session, coordinating materials, training teen volunteers, and facilitating all workshops.

This artifact demonstrates my ability to design and implement engaging, collaborative learning experiences grounded in educational theory. The workshop structure reflects constructivist learning principles by encouraging children to explore and build their own understanding through experimentation and guided discovery. Sessions were intentionally structured to reduce cognitive overload—each one focused on a single scientific topic, with clear goals and simple, manageable steps in line with cognitive load theory. This thoughtful instructional approach supported both individual and group learning, promoted inquiry, and offered a welcoming environment for young learners to engage with STEM topics in a public library context.

Assignment 3: Do It Yourself or Maker’s Programming (INFO 260A)

As part of an assignment for my Programming and Services for Children (INFO 260A) course, I designed a conceptual library program titled "Coraline’s Adventure: A Journey Through Imagination!"—a DIY/Maker event for children and tweens that uses crafts, games, and storytelling to bring the world of Coraline to life. Though not yet implemented, the program is grounded in key learning theories, particularly constructivism and cognitive load theory. Participants would engage in hands-on, creative activities like crafting themed items and participating in a treasure hunt, allowing them to construct knowledge through active exploration and personal connection to the story. The event is structured into clear, manageable tasks to avoid overwhelming participants, supporting cognitive load theory by helping young learners focus on one activity at a time.

Social interaction is built into the design through collaborative stations where participants could work together, share ideas, and support each other’s creativity—reflecting Vygotsky’s emphasis on the importance of peer interaction in learning. While still a proposed plan, this program demonstrates my ability to design learner-centered, theory-informed programming that supports both individual and collaborative learning experiences, and highlights the role of libraries in building creativity, imagination, and educational play.

Tire Tactics (INFO 281)

For an assignment in my Teaching Life Skills in a Public Library (INFO 281) course, we were told to research and create a life skill related program for a public library. Tire Tactics is the hands-on library program I designed to teach teens and young adults how to repair a flat tire and perform basic car maintenance. Informed by learning principles such as constructivism and cognitive load theory, the workshop was structured to support both individual and collaborative learning. The session was broken into manageable segments—like identifying tools, following a step-by-step tire change demonstration, and practicing the process on a demo vehicle—to reduce cognitive overload and help participants focus on one task at a time. Visual aids and hands-on guidance allowed learners to build understanding through direct experience, reflecting constructivist principles of active, contextual learning.

The program also focuses on collaborative learning by encouraging participants to work in pairs or small groups during practice activities, promoting peer-to-peer support and shared problem-solving. These interactions would help reinforce key concepts while developing communication and teamwork skills, aligning with Johnson and Johnson’s cooperative learning model. Tire Tactics demonstrates my ability to design meaningful, learner-centered programs rooted in instructional theory and shows how public libraries can provide practical, engaging learning experiences that empower users with real-world skills.

Conclusion

As I continue my journey toward becoming a librarian, the principles of constructivism, cognitive load theory, and collaborative learning continue to inform how I approach instructional design. Although I first encountered these theories during my undergraduate years, where I applied them in a medical and health context, seeing and applying them in a library setting has been a new and exciting challenge. In healthcare, these theories were useful for patient education and training, but I’ve come to realize how powerful they can be in the context of library services, where the goal is to engage individuals in lifelong learning and discovery. Whether in one-on-one interactions or group settings, these theories help me create experiences that meet the diverse needs of library patrons and provide opportunities for meaningful learning. I believe that by focusing on these principles, libraries can offer more than just access to information—they can create spaces where individuals grow, connect, and thrive.
