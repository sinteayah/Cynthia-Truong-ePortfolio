Competency N

Evaluate programs and services using measurable criteria.

Introduction

The effectiveness of library programs can only be true through the evaluation and assessment that shapes it. When designing library services and initiatives, a well-structured evaluation process allows professionals to make informed, data-driven decisions that highlight a program’s impact with the community. By analyzing outcomes, libraries can determine whether their initiatives align with organizational goals, successfully engage their target audiences, and address the unique needs of their communities. Without ongoing evaluation, libraries risk falling into outdated practices based on tradition rather than evidence, which can hinder their ability to meet evolving community needs (Matthews, 2017).

In addition to program development, evaluation is an essential part of library marketing and advocacy strategies as it helps measure whether a program is achieving its intended results or if adjustments are necessary. Evaluation also serves as a powerful tool for communicating the value of library services to stakeholders by demonstrating their contributions. For example, circulation statistics or reference transaction data provide measurable performance indicators that help libraries justify resource allocation and highlight their impact on the community (Cassell & Hiremath, 2018). Moreover, evaluation is particularly critical when developing inclusive services, such as programs for patrons with disabilities. By gathering and analyzing feedback, libraries can identify gaps in accessibility and make informed improvements to enhance services, making sure they are equitable and effective for all users (Lazar & Briggs, 2015).

Measurable Criteria

When designing an evaluation strategy, it is essential to establish measurable criteria that provide an accurate assessment of a program’s performance. Measurable criteria include clear benchmarks or standards that help libraries determine the success or areas of improvement for their services. Rossi et al. (2004) outline a general framework for evaluation that includes setting criteria of merit, defining performance standards, measuring outcomes, and synthesizing data to form a comprehensive judgment. In a public library setting, these steps help determine whether programs effectively serve the community and meet organizational goals.

Evaluation questions should align with stakeholder interests and focus on key aspects of program performance. A well-constructed question establishes a clear standard and explains why meeting that standard is important for the program’s success (Wildemuth, 2016). For example, asking, "Is at least 75% of program participants part of the intended audience?" directly connects program outcomes with measurable performance criteria. This approach allows evaluation data to be actionable and supports future decisions related to resource allocation, program adjustments, or advocacy efforts to highlight the library’s impact (Cassell & Hiremath, 2018).

Data Collection Methods

Collecting data is a key step in evaluating the effectiveness of library programs. Surveys are among the most commonly used tools for gathering user feedback because they capture personal opinions and preferences from a wide audience (Connaway & Powell, 2010). While they may not always provide the most in-depth analysis, surveys can offer valuable quantitative insights into community interests, perceptions, and participation. When a representative sample is selected, survey results can be generalized to reflect broader trends, giving libraries a clearer picture of a program’s impact. However, surveys also have their limitations, such as response bias, where non-respondents might have answered differently, leading to skewed results (Fink, 2013). The sampling method used in surveys plays an important role in making sure the sample is representative of the larger population. Common sampling techniques include random sampling, where each individual has an equal chance of being selected, and stratified sampling, which divides the population into subgroups to reflect key characteristics (Connaway & Powell, 2010). The choice of sampling method influences the reliability and validity of the results, therefore making it an important consideration in data collection.

Other data collection methods, including interviews, focus groups, and observations, provide alternative ways to gather user input. Interviews and focus groups offer deeper qualitative insights but require more time and resources, and their findings may not always apply to a larger audience (Krueger & Casey, 2015). Observations allow evaluators to assess engagement in real time but may not capture the full range of user experiences. The best method depends on the goals of the evaluation and the type of data needed to guide decision-making (Creswell & Creswell, 2018).

Evidence

Assignment #2 Variables, Measures & Sampling (INFO 285)

For my Applied Research Methods course (INFO 285), Assignment #3, I developed a program evaluation plan for one of the case examples provided, the University Library Building Use Study. I identified four key variables to assess library services and spaces: Library Space Utilization (measuring the use of different library areas), Frequency of Library Visits (tracking how often visitors come to the library each month), Technology Use in the Library (examining the usage of technological resources like Wi-Fi and computers), and Study Room Reservation (counting the number of study room bookings per month). These variables were operationalized through specific survey questions, such as “Which areas of the library have you used in the past week?” for space utilization and “How often do you visit the library in a typical month?” for visit frequency. Data was collected using nominal, ordinal, and ratio scales depending on the nature of the variable.

Additionally, I created a sampling strategy by selecting a convenience sampling method to target library visitors who had used the spaces and technologies within the past month. The evaluation plan planned to survey respondents both digitally and in-person in high-traffic areas of the library. This approach helped gather a manageable sample while being mindful of time and resource constraints.

This assignment highlights my ability to evaluate programs using clear, measurable criteria. By defining specific variables and selecting appropriate data collection methods, I was able to design a plan that provides actionable insights into the library's usage and the effectiveness of its services. The alignment between the survey questions and the evaluation variables allows for meaningful data that can inform improvements. The sampling strategy further supports the integrity of the evaluation by capturing a diverse range of responses while maintaining practical feasibility. Overall, this assignment demonstrates my competence in applying systematic, evidence-based methods to evaluate a program, fulfilling the objectives of Competency N.

Discussion #2: Information Visualization Practices and Applications in Libraries (INFO 246)

For my second piece of evidence, I refer to a discussion post from my Information Visualization course (INFO 246), where I examined the application of information visualization technologies, such as Gimlet, in public libraries to assess and enhance services. In this discussion, I explored how Gimlet tracks and categorizes reference transactions—such as patron interactions and question types—and how this data is transformed into visual reports. These visualizations enable library staff to identify usage trends, evaluate service performance, and make informed decisions about staffing, resource allocation, and service adjustments.

This artifact directly demonstrates Competency N by illustrating my ability to apply measurable criteria to evaluate library services. I highlighted how visualized data—such as transaction types, interaction lengths, and service frequency—can be used to track library service patterns and pinpoint areas for improvement. For example, using tools like Microsoft Power BI to visualize data from Gimlet helps library staff identify peak service times and assess whether additional personnel or resources are required. By focusing on service-specific criteria, such as staffing needs and resource distribution, I emphasized how data visualization contributes to improving library operations and service effectiveness. This directly aligns with the competency’s objective of evaluating professional activities and services based on measurable data.

Peer Review- Evaluation of Exchange Group's Database (INFO 202)

The third piece of evidence is a peer review assignment from INFO 202, where I evaluated the design, functionality, and effectiveness of a database developed by an Exchange Group. My role in this project was to apply measurable criteria to assess the clarity, consistency, and usability of the database. I identified areas for improvement, such as clarifying ambiguous terms like “individual consumption” and recommending the inclusion of vendor location information. Additionally, I suggested changes to improve the user experience, such as incorporating visual aids and reorganizing the rules to better align with the submission form.

By offering feedback grounded in concrete standards—such as clarity, usability, and consistency—I illustrated how applying measurable criteria can lead to meaningful improvements. My recommendations aimed to enhance both the database’s functionality and its user experience, underscoring the importance of clear, actionable insights in service evaluation. This peer review not only allowed me to contribute to the refinement of a professional information system but also helped reinforce my ability to assess and improve services based on observable, measurable standards. In doing so, I strengthened my skills in evaluating information systems and services, making sure that my feedback aligns with the competency's goal of using clear, evidence-based criteria for continuous service improvement.


Conclusion

The effective evaluation of library programs and services is indispensable for keeping libraries responsive to the ever-changing needs of their communities and staff. By applying measurable criteria, libraries can assess whether a program achieves its goals, supports the institution’s mission, and produces meaningful outcomes for users. Data-driven insights from tools such as surveys, reference tracking systems, and information visualizations help identify areas that need improvement, optimize resource allocation, and elevate the quality of services offered.

Moreover, evaluation is not just about reviewing current programs—it's essential for shaping future initiatives and demonstrating the library’s ongoing impact. As Lazar and Briggs (2015) note, regular evaluations of accessibility services allow libraries to enhance inclusivity, making sure patrons with print disabilities have equitable access to services. Tools like Gimlet, which track reference transactions, provide important data that helps libraries assess service usage and guide improvements (Chan & Johns-Masten, 2014). By using robust evaluation processes, libraries can make data-backed decisions, refine their offerings, and make sure they meet community needs more effectively.

For public libraries, evaluation is particularly valuable in aligning services with community expectations and building engagement. With limited resources, libraries must make strategic decisions on how to best serve diverse populations and the demographic of the community. Whether focusing on outreach programs for underserved communities or innovating digital literacy services, evaluation assists libraries to make informed decisions, advocate for their value, and continuously adapt to meet the evolving information needs of the public. Through thoughtful and consistent evaluation practices, public libraries can uphold their critical role as community hubs—offering valuable programs, demonstrating their worth to stakeholders, and supporting lifelong learning and inclusivity. In sum, evaluation is not just a tool for assessing past performance but a cornerstone for continuous growth and excellence in public library services.
